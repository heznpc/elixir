King et al. (2020) over-pathologization criteria for screening-tool face validity
("Face validity evaluation of screening tools for gaming disorder: Scope, language,
and overpathologizing issues", Journal of Behavioral Addictions, 9(1), 1-13).

Each criterion is applied to a single candidate scale item. The model returns
"violates" if the item is judged to fail the criterion, and "ok" if not.

---

CRITERION 1 - SCOPE
The item must describe behavior that is plausibly outside the range of normal,
healthy engagement with the medium. If a typical adult gamer with no clinical
hoarding-related symptoms would, at a moderate level of agreement, endorse the
item simply by virtue of being a typical engaged player, the item violates the
SCOPE criterion. Concretely, the item violates SCOPE if it describes a behavior
that is essentially universal among engaged players (e.g., "I sometimes save
items in my inventory") with no marker of impairment, distress, frequency, or
loss of control.

The item does NOT violate SCOPE if it includes any of:
- A frequency, duration, or escalation marker that distinguishes it from normal
  play ("more than an hour in a single session", "more than I planned",
  "even when the use was strategically sound").
- An explicit distress / impairment marker (causes guilt, anxiety, interferes
  with rent / food / bills, interferes with enjoyment of other games).
- A loss-of-control marker ("continued spending", "cannot stop", "interferes
  with").

---

CRITERION 2 - LANGUAGE
The item's wording must not borrow clinical-disorder language (addiction,
compulsion, withdrawal, craving, obsession, pathology) without empirical
warrant. King et al. (2020) explicitly warn that importing substance-use
language into game behavior pathologizes normal play. The item violates the
LANGUAGE criterion if it uses such clinical-disorder vocabulary at the level
of the stem itself, OR if its wording presupposes a disorder ("I am addicted
to ...", "I obsessively ...", "I compulsively ...").

The item does NOT violate LANGUAGE merely by describing strong feelings
(anxiety, guilt, distress, loss) when those feelings are reported as
consequences of behavior rather than diagnostic labels attached to the
behavior itself.

---

CRITERION 3 - OVERPATHOLOGIZING
The item must distinguish behavior that is normatively rational given the
designed environment of the game from behavior that exceeds that environment.
Saving consumables for harder fights is normatively rational when information
about future challenges is incomplete. Buying a game during a sale is
normatively rational when prices may rise later. The item violates the
OVERPATHOLOGIZING criterion if a normatively rational behavior is framed as
inherently problematic, OR if it asks the respondent to label adaptive
behavior (sensible resource management, sale purchasing, achievement
engagement) as a symptom.

The item does NOT violate OVERPATHOLOGIZING if the symptom-like aspect of the
behavior is itself the explicit subject (distress, impairment, loss of
control, escalation beyond planned commitment).

---

VERDICT
For each (item, criterion) pair, emit exactly one of:
  - "violates"   if the item fails the criterion
  - "ok"         if the item passes the criterion

Plus a short, single-sentence rationale (<= 25 words).
